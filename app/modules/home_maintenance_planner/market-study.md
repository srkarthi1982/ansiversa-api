## Purpose

This study captures the market context for a private home maintenance planning tool. It informs product direction without copying competitor wording, UI, schedules, templates, or proprietary workflows.

## Problem Statement

People often remember home maintenance only after something feels urgent. Recurring jobs such as air-conditioner servicing, filter cleaning, pest control, water-tank cleaning, exterior checks, and appliance care can be scattered across calendars, notes, messages, and paper invoices. The result is missed due dates, unclear provider history, and weak visibility into estimated versus actual costs.

## Target Users

- Renters who need reminders for appliance care and minor upkeep.
- Homeowners and villa residents managing seasonal or recurring maintenance.
- Families coordinating shared household responsibilities.
- Landlords managing their own residence or a small number of personal properties.
- People who want records of service providers, reference numbers, and costs.

## Competitor Landscape

- Calendar apps such as Google Calendar, Apple Calendar, and Outlook support recurring reminders but do not model maintenance areas, categories, costs, and service details together.
- Task managers such as Todoist, TickTick, Microsoft To Do, and Any.do handle recurring tasks but are general-purpose and can lose home-specific context.
- Home inventory and property apps sometimes include maintenance records, but many are heavier than a personal planner needs.
- Spreadsheet templates are flexible but require manual formulas, recurring-date discipline, and consistent data entry.

## Common Market Features

- Recurring reminders and due dates.
- Task lists grouped by room or system.
- Priority labels and completion tracking.
- Service provider and invoice/reference notes.
- Cost estimates and actual spend.
- Basic dashboard views for overdue and upcoming work.

## User Love Signals

Users value low-friction entry, predictable recurring reminders, and a clear view of what is due soon. They also appreciate keeping provider names, phone numbers, and invoice numbers near the task instead of searching old messages.

## Complaints And Friction

Common friction includes overbuilt property-management tools, confusing recurrence behavior, too many required fields, poor mobile entry, and unclear separation between completed recurring work and the next occurrence.

## Pricing And Paywall Observations

General task and calendar tools often provide free basic recurrence but reserve advanced reminders, collaboration, or history for paid tiers. Property-management software targets professionals and can be too expensive or complex for personal use.

## AI Trends

AI appears in some property and task tools for suggestion generation, but Home Maintenance Planner should not add AI diagnosis or safety advice. The V1 should stay deterministic, private, and manually controlled.

## UX Patterns Worth Studying

- Simple task creation forms with optional advanced fields.
- Filter chips for overdue, due this week, priority, category, and area.
- Clear recurring schedule labels.
- Completion actions that visibly move a recurring task to its next due date.
- Compact dashboards with practical counts and cost totals.

## Ansiversa Differentiation

Ansiversa can offer a focused private planner that sits beside other household tools without becoming a contractor marketplace, inspection tool, or property-management system. The value is in owner-scoped records, practical recurrence, and clear manual verification.

## Demand Assessment

The problem is common and recurring across renters, families, and homeowners. Demand is likely strongest for users already managing household responsibilities in notes, calendars, or chat threads.

## Privacy Considerations

Maintenance records can include home routines, provider contacts, costs, and reference numbers. Data must be authenticated and owner-scoped. No public sharing, external contractor integration, or notification provider should be added without explicit approval.

## Safety Considerations

The app is a planning and record-keeping tool. It must not claim professional inspection, guaranteed safety, regulatory compliance, emergency repair service, or contractor certification. Electrical, structural, gas, fire-safety, and hazardous work should be referred to qualified professionals.

## Scope Boundaries

- No emergency repair dispatch.
- No contractor marketplace.
- No diagnosis of property faults.
- No file uploads or invoice storage in V1.
- No AI recommendations.
- No regulatory compliance claims.

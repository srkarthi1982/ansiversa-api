## Purpose

This study frames First Aid Guide as an educational reference app. It is not emergency dispatch, diagnosis, medication advice, personalized treatment, or a substitute for professionals.

## Problem Statement

People want simple first-aid reference topics before stressful situations happen. Many resources are either too clinical, too long, or unclear about when emergency services should be contacted.

## Target Users

- Individuals preparing household reference material.
- Families who want easy-to-revisit educational topics.
- Users building basic emergency preparedness habits.

## Competitor Landscape

Alternatives include public first-aid websites, Red Cross-style training resources, emergency-preparedness checklists, health portals, and general search. The strongest products are clear, concise, and explicit about emergency limits.

## Common Market Features

- Topic browsing.
- Category filters.
- Step-by-step educational guidance.
- Emergency warning notes.
- Prevention tips.
- Favourites and recent views.

## User Love Signals

Users value plain language, offline-like organization, fast search, clear warnings, and concise steps.

## Complaints And Friction

Users distrust vague medical claims, confusing instructions, paywalls around safety information, and apps that blur education with treatment advice.

## AI Trends

Some products summarize or personalize advice. First Aid Guide V1 excludes AI-generated advice and personalization to preserve safety boundaries.

## Opportunities

- Calm educational reference inside Ansiversa.
- Strong disclaimers and emergency boundaries.
- Personal bookmarks and viewing history without medical inference.

## Avoid List

- Diagnosis.
- Medication recommendation.
- Personalized treatment.
- Emergency dispatch.
- Claims that the app replaces emergency services or healthcare professionals.

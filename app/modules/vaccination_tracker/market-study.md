## Purpose

This study frames Vaccination Tracker as personal record keeping for profiles, vaccination records, dose series, and upcoming due dates. It is not a medical recommendation engine, official certificate system, or clinical scheduling tool.

## Problem Definition

Families and adults often store vaccination information across paper cards, clinic portals, government apps, photos, email, and memory. The problem is organization, retrieval, and follow-up visibility, not deciding medical appropriateness.

## Target Users

- Parents organizing child vaccination history.
- Adults recording annual, travel, workplace, or clinic-administered vaccines.
- Families reviewing upcoming and overdue doses before contacting a qualified provider.

## Existing Alternatives

Alternatives include government immunization records, clinic portals, pharmacy apps, Apple Health/Google Health-style records, travel vaccine paperwork, spreadsheets, and note-taking apps.

## Competitor Patterns

Common patterns include profile-based records, vaccine names, dose numbers, due dates, provider/batch/reference fields, reminders, certificate storage, and official integration in government systems.

## Pain Points

Users struggle with scattered records, unclear next-dose dates, lost paper cards, family-member separation, and uncertainty over whether app data is official. Privacy and sensitive health data handling matter.

## Ansiversa Differentiation

Ansiversa can provide a private, lightweight organizer that avoids clinical claims and keeps records owner-scoped, searchable, and easy to review.

## Medical Safety Considerations

The app must not recommend vaccines, prescribe schedules, guarantee immunity, or replace healthcare professionals and official vaccination authorities.

## Scope Boundaries

No AI, no hospital/pharmacy/government integrations, no official certificates, no attachment uploads in V1, and no automatic universal schedules.

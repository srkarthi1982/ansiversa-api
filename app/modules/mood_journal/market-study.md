## Purpose

This document captures market intelligence for Mood Journal so future product
decisions can be grounded in public competitor patterns, user pain points, and
Ansiversa's platform direction.

This is research only. It does not copy competitor wording, mood scales, prompts,
clinical models, UI, insight systems, or proprietary workflows, and it does not
recommend immediate implementation.

## Problem Statement

People want to understand emotional patterns, triggers, activities, sleep,
stress, relationships, and daily context, but journaling can be hard to sustain.
The problem is not only recording mood. Users need quick check-ins, private
reflection, useful pattern recognition, and safe boundaries that do not turn an
app into an unqualified therapist.

The market spans mood trackers, journals, CBT-inspired apps, self-care
companions, mental health tools, and AI reflection apps. Trust depends on
privacy, clarity, and responsible mental-health positioning.

## Target Users

- Individuals tracking emotional patterns.
- Users trying to understand triggers and routines.
- People who prefer quick mood check-ins over long journaling.
- Users reflecting on stress, sleep, work, relationships, or habits.
- Students and professionals managing daily emotional awareness.
- Wellness and Goal Planner users adding emotional context.
- Users preparing notes for therapy or coaching conversations.
- Privacy-conscious users journaling sensitive feelings.

## Competitor Landscape

### Direct Competitors

- Daylio: Popular mood and activity tracker with quick check-ins, statistics,
  goals, reminders, and journaling without requiring long writing.
- Moodnotes: Mood tracking and CBT-informed thought journaling, focused on
  identifying thinking patterns and improving self-awareness.
- Bearable: Health and mood tracker connecting symptoms, mood, sleep,
  medication, habits, and factors.
- How We Feel: Free emotion-tracking app backed by emotional intelligence
  research, with detailed emotion labeling and context.
- Reflectly and Journey: Journaling apps with mood tracking, prompts, and
  reflection workflows.
- Moodfit, Moodgrade, Rohy AI, DailyBean, eMoods, and similar tools: Compete
  across mood tracking, AI insights, mental-health journaling, symptoms, and
  pattern analysis.

### Indirect Competitors

- Paper journals and bullet journals.
- Apple Journal, Day One, Notion, and general journaling apps.
- Meditation and mindfulness apps such as Calm and Headspace.
- Therapy apps and mental-health platforms.
- Habit trackers and wellness planners.
- Wearables and health apps that infer stress or mood-adjacent signals.
- AI chat companions and reflection bots.

### AI-Based Alternatives

- ChatGPT and Claude can guide reflection, summarize journal entries, and help
  identify patterns, but privacy and mental-health boundaries are serious
  concerns.
- AI journaling apps increasingly offer sentiment analysis, prompt generation,
  and pattern insights.
- AI companions may provide emotional support, but risk overdependency and
  unclear clinical boundaries.

AI assistants compete around reflection and insight. Dedicated mood journals win
when they are private, consistent, easy to use, and responsible about limits.

## Common Market Features

- Mood rating or emotion selection.
- Activity, factor, and trigger tagging.
- Text journal entries.
- Prompted reflection.
- Calendar and streak views.
- Charts and trends.
- Sleep, medication, symptom, or habit correlation.
- Reminders.
- Export for personal review or clinician discussion.
- Privacy lock and local/offline options.
- AI insights or summaries in newer tools.
- Crisis or support-resource disclaimers in responsible mental health apps.

## What Users Appear to Love

- Fast check-ins that are easy to sustain.
- Seeing patterns across mood, activities, and sleep.
- Private journaling space.
- Gentle prompts that help reflection.
- Visual trends and calendars.
- Custom mood labels or activities.
- Exporting notes for therapy or self-review.
- Apps that avoid judgment and pressure.

## Common Complaints / Friction

- Too many prompts can feel burdensome.
- Streaks can make emotional tracking feel like another chore.
- Mood scales may oversimplify complex feelings.
- AI insights may feel inaccurate or intrusive.
- Mental-health apps can overclaim or blur therapy boundaries.
- Sensitive data privacy is a major concern.
- Free tiers may restrict history, insights, export, or customization.
- Users may stop tracking if insights do not lead to action.
- Clinical assessments require careful handling.

## Pricing and Paywall Observations

- Daylio, Bearable, Moodfit, Reflectly, Journey, and similar apps commonly use
  freemium or subscription models for advanced statistics, customization,
  backup, export, and insights.
- How We Feel is free, creating a strong accessible baseline for emotion
  tracking.
- General journaling apps monetize sync, media, templates, AI prompts, and
  cross-device access.
- AI mood/journal apps monetize insights and guided reflection.

Users may pay for privacy, useful insights, and export, but mental-health data
paywalls require careful trust handling.

## AI Capability Trends

- AI can summarize journal patterns and suggest reflection prompts.
- Sentiment and emotion analysis are becoming common but imperfect.
- Apps increasingly combine mood, symptoms, habits, sleep, and medication.
- Privacy-first and offline modes are important differentiators.
- Mental-health safety boundaries and crisis-resource handling matter.
- AI companions raise concerns around dependency and unqualified advice.

AI should support self-reflection and pattern recognition, not diagnosis,
treatment, or crisis counseling.

## UX Patterns Worth Studying

- Two-tap mood check-in.
- Optional journal text after mood selection.
- Custom activities/factors.
- Calendar and trend view.
- Gentle prompt library.
- Privacy lock and export/delete controls.
- Clear "not therapy" boundary.
- Pattern insights with source data visible.
- Low-pressure reminders.
- Weekly reflection summary.

## Opportunities for Ansiversa

- Position Mood Journal as private emotional awareness and reflection, not
  therapy.
- Connect naturally with Wellness and Goal Planner, Goal Tracker, Study Planner,
  Sleep/water/fitness future apps, and Doctor Visit Tracker through approved
  platform boundaries.
- Keep check-ins fast and optional.
- Make data export/delete and privacy clear.
- Prefer gentle pattern review over clinical claims.
- Allow users to connect mood context to wellness goals only with consent.

## What Ansiversa Should Avoid

- Do not copy competitor mood scales, emotion maps, prompts, UI, clinical
  models, or insight systems.
- Do not diagnose, treat, or claim mental-health outcomes.
- Do not make users feel punished for missing entries.
- Do not store mood data without strong privacy controls.
- Do not provide crisis counseling through AI.
- Do not overstate sentiment or pattern accuracy.
- Do not add global abstractions or shared components from this research alone.

## Product Questions for Future Review

- Should Mood Journal use simple mood scale, emotion labels, or both?
- Should journal text be optional after check-in?
- Should AI insights be excluded initially?
- What privacy lock/export/delete controls are required?
- Should mood data connect to Wellness and Goal Planner?
- Should clinical questionnaires be out of scope?
- What support-resource language is appropriate?
- How should trends avoid implying diagnosis?

## Purpose, problem, and target users

People comparing consequential options often hold criteria and trade-offs in scattered notes. Individuals and small-business users benefit from a transparent matrix without surrendering judgment to an opaque recommendation.

## Landscape and common patterns

Decision-matrix tools commonly provide options, weighted criteria, scores, rankings, and exports. Users value clarity and reusable structure but report friction from forced 100-point weights, wide spreadsheets, unexplained scores, and claims that a calculated winner is inherently correct. AI trends generate criteria and recommendations, which are outside this product boundary.

## Ansiversa opportunity and UX direction

Use proportional normalization, explicit higher/lower direction, visible incomplete ratings, mobile option cards, honest ties, neutral selection, and reflection. Avoid random-choice mechanics, advice, external pricing/research, collaboration, hidden transformations, and guaranteed-outcome language.

## Product questions

Destination approval, exports, templates, and any future scoring snapshots remain open decisions.

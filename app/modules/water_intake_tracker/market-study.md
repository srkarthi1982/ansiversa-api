## Purpose

This study frames Water Intake Tracker as a personal hydration logging tool. It is not a medical device, clinical hydration assessment, diagnosis tool, or prescription engine.

## Problem Statement

People often want a lightweight way to remember how much water they consumed, compare it with their own goal, and see whether daily routines are consistent. Existing hydration apps commonly compete on reminders, wearable integrations, gamification, and streaks, but many users need a simpler private log.

## Target Users

- Individuals building a hydration habit.
- Wellness-oriented users who prefer manual records.
- People who want simple daily, weekly, and monthly summaries.

## Competitor Landscape

Common alternatives include Apple Health manual water records, Google Fit-compatible trackers, WaterMinder, My Water, Hydro Coach, habit trackers, and spreadsheet/manual note systems.

## Common Market Features

- Daily goal and consumed amount.
- Quick add buttons for common quantities.
- Drink type labels.
- Streaks and simple trend charts.
- Reminders and wearable integrations in more advanced products.

## User Love Signals

Users tend to value fast logging, clear progress, flexible units, and history that is easy to correct later.

## Complaints And Friction

Common friction includes noisy reminders, excessive health claims, complicated setup, paywalls around basic statistics, and confusing unit handling.

## Opportunities For Ansiversa

- Keep V1 focused on private manual logging.
- Make unit conversion transparent.
- Keep goals user-managed rather than recommended.
- Avoid medical claims and wearable dependency.

## Avoid List

- Do not diagnose dehydration.
- Do not prescribe intake targets.
- Do not claim health outcomes.
- Do not imply clinical monitoring.
- Do not add wearable integrations in V1.

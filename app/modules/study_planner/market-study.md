## Purpose

This document captures market intelligence for Study Planner so future product
decisions can be grounded in public competitor patterns, student organization
pain points, planning behavior, AI trends, and Ansiversa's platform direction.

This is research only. It does not copy competitor wording, UI, templates,
planner layouts, or proprietary flows, and it does not recommend immediate
implementation.

## Problem Statement

Students and self-learners struggle to convert classes, assignments, exams,
revision goals, and personal commitments into a realistic plan. A calendar
shows time, a task list shows work, and notes show material, but learners need a
study-specific workflow that connects deadlines, sessions, progress, and
revision priorities.

The market problem is not simply "remember tasks." It is helping users reduce
overwhelm, plan study time realistically, and maintain momentum without creating
another complex system to manage.

## Target Users

- High school and college students managing classes, assignments, and exams.
- Exam candidates planning revision across multiple subjects.
- Online learners balancing work, family, and study.
- Parents supporting student organization.
- Tutors helping learners create study schedules.
- Self-learners preparing certifications or personal learning goals.
- Students who already use calendars/task apps but need academic context.

## Competitor Landscape

### Direct Competitors

- MyStudyLife: Student planner focused on classes, homework, exams, reminders,
  rotating timetables, study tracking, and cross-device sync.
- Power Planner: Homework and grade planner with reminders, semester support,
  grade calculation, calendar sync, and a low-cost lifetime premium model.
- Notion student planner templates: Highly customizable student dashboards for
  assignments, exams, schedules, notes, habits, and goals.
- Todoist: General task manager often adapted by students for due dates,
  recurring study tasks, projects, labels, filters, reminders, and calendar
  views.
- Google Calendar / Apple Calendar / Outlook Calendar: Time-blocking and
  reminder tools commonly used for classes and study sessions.
- Habit trackers and Pomodoro apps: Indirectly support study routines through
  focus sessions and streaks.

### Indirect Competitors

- LMS calendars such as Canvas, Moodle, Google Classroom, and Blackboard.
- Paper planners and printable study timetables.
- Spreadsheets used for exam revision plans.
- Notes apps where users maintain manual to-do lists.
- Course-specific tools that show assignments and deadlines but not a full
  personal study plan.

### AI-Based Alternatives

- ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and Notion AI can generate study timetables,
  revision plans, spaced repetition schedules, and exam preparation checklists.
- AI assistants are flexible, but users must manually maintain the plan and
  update progress unless the AI is connected to structured records.

## Common Market Features

- Classes, subjects, or courses.
- Assignment and homework tracking.
- Exam and test date tracking.
- Timetables, rotating schedules, and calendar views.
- Study sessions and revision blocks.
- Reminders and notifications.
- Grade tracking or GPA calculation.
- Progress indicators and completion status.
- Cross-device sync.
- Calendar integration.
- Pomodoro or focus timer.
- Notes or links attached to tasks.
- Templates for semesters, courses, or revision cycles.

## What Users Appear to Love

- Seeing all classes, deadlines, and exams in one place.
- Reminders that prevent missed homework.
- Grade tracking and "what do I need to score" planning.
- Calendar sync with due dates.
- Low-friction entry of assignments and exams.
- A planner designed for student life instead of generic work tasks.
- Simple free tiers or low one-time purchases.
- Notion-style customization when users enjoy building their own system.

## Common Complaints / Friction

- Planners can become too much work to maintain.
- Notion templates can be visually appealing but overwhelming.
- Generic task managers do not understand semesters, classes, grades, or exams.
- Free tiers may limit semesters, reminders, projects, or grade entries.
- Students may abandon planners if data entry takes longer than the task itself.
- AI-generated plans can be unrealistic if they do not know actual workload and
  energy patterns.
- Notifications can become noise.
- Sync reliability and calendar integration are critical trust points.

## Pricing and Paywall Observations

- MyStudyLife publicly positions its planner as free while offering extra
  features and promotions such as MSL+.
- Power Planner emphasizes a fully usable free tier and a low-cost one-time
  lifetime premium purchase, which users appear to appreciate in a subscription
  heavy market.
- Todoist has a free Beginner plan and paid Pro/Business tiers; reminders,
  larger project limits, and advanced organization can push users toward Pro.
- Notion templates range from free to paid marketplace products; the platform
  itself has broader pricing around collaboration, AI, and workspace scale.
- Students are price-sensitive and respond positively to transparent, low-cost,
  or genuinely useful free options.

## AI Capability Trends

- AI planners can generate study schedules from exams, topics, and available
  time.
- AI can break goals into sessions, estimate workload, and rebalance missed
  sessions.
- AI can connect study plans to notes, quizzes, and spaced repetition.
- Trust depends on making AI plans editable and realistic.
- The future direction is adaptive planning: plans respond to progress,
  confidence, deadlines, and missed work rather than staying static.

## UX Patterns Worth Studying

- Start with term/semester setup, then add courses and deadlines.
- Show "today," "this week," and "upcoming exams" clearly.
- Let users plan backward from exam dates.
- Keep assignment entry fast on mobile.
- Use calm reminders and priority cues.
- Separate schedule, tasks, exams, and progress without fragmenting the app.
- Provide drag-and-drop or quick reschedule for missed sessions.
- Show completion and workload balance in plain language.
- Avoid requiring a perfect setup before value appears.

## Opportunities for Ansiversa

- Build a study-specific planner that stays calmer than generic productivity
  suites.
- Connect Study Planner with Quiz, AI Notes Summarizer, Course Tracker, Lesson
  Builder, Memory Trainer, and Daily Word Challenge through approved APIs.
- Focus on realistic planning: deadlines, session length, available days, and
  review rhythm.
- Preserve student-owned plans, sessions, tasks, and reflections as long-lived
  records.
- Add future AI as "plan suggestion" rather than automatic schedule authority.
- Provide recovery workflows for missed study sessions without guilt-heavy
  design.
- Keep pricing/limits transparent for students.

## What Ansiversa Should Avoid

- Do not copy planner templates, dashboards, calendar layouts, or proprietary
  onboarding flows.
- Do not make planning so complex that it becomes another assignment.
- Do not over-notify users or create anxiety-driven streak mechanics.
- Do not produce AI plans that imply guaranteed academic outcomes.
- Do not silently inspect notes, grades, or course data for AI planning.
- Do not turn Study Planner into a full LMS without Partner/Astra approval.
- Do not require heavy setup before the user can plan the first study session.

## Product Questions for Future Review

- Should Study Planner center on sessions, assignments, exams, or goals?
- Should grade tracking be part of Study Planner or stay in Course Tracker?
- Should AI planning require user confirmation before creating sessions?
- Which calendar integration is most valuable for Ansiversa users?
- Should missed sessions trigger automatic replanning or a user-guided review?
- Should Study Planner support rotating timetables?
- How should the app handle students versus working professionals?
